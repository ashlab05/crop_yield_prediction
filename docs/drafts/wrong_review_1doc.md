A Hybrid Deep Learning Architecture for Robust Crop Yield Prediction: Combining Transformers, GNNs, COATI Optimization, and XAI






 
Abstract - Accurate crop yield prediction is a critical component of global food security, agricultural planning, and climate resilience, particularly under increasing climate variability and resource constraints. Traditional statistical and machine learning approaches have demonstrated promising performance on historical agricultural datasets; however, these models often rely on country-specific patterns and fail to generalize when deployed in regions with limited or no historical data. This lack of spatial generalization significantly limits their real-world applicability, especially for developing countries and data-scarce regions. To address this challenge, this paper proposes a climate-similarity–driven Graph Neural Network (GNN) framework for crop yield prediction that explicitly targets out-of-country generalization. Countries are modeled as graph nodes, with edges constructed based on climate similarity using temperature and rainfall features, enabling structured knowledge transfer across regions with comparable agro-climatic conditions. The proposed approach is evaluated using a strict Leave-Country-Out (LCO) validation protocol, ensuring that test countries are completely unseen during training. Experimental results demonstrate that the Climate GNN achieves a Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of 33,317 and an R² score of 0.6759 on unseen countries, outperforming traditional machine learning models and non-climate-aware graph baselines by a significant margin. A key methodological contribution of this work is the application of log normalization to crop yield values, which stabilizes gradient flow, mitigates the impact of extreme outliers, and enables effective learning across a wide yield range. In addition, the Coati Optimization Algorithm (COATI), a bio-inspired metaheuristic, is employed for hyperparameter optimization of the GNN architecture. While COATI provides marginal improvements in in-distribution performance, extensive analysis reveals a critical trade-off: COATI-enhanced models exhibit degraded performance under strict geographic domain shifts, highlighting the risk of overfitting to seen-country patterns. This finding uncovers an important insight into the balance between semantic enrichment and spatial generalization in graph-based learning. To enhance transparency and scientific validity, SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) are applied to a baseline multilayer perceptron model, confirming that temperature, rainfall, and pesticide usage exhibit biologically plausible and interpretable relationships with crop yield. Overall, this study demonstrates that climate-similarity GNNs provide a robust and transferable solution for crop yield prediction across unseen regions, while also offering a critical evaluation of optimization-driven model enhancements under real-world deployment constraints.
Keywords— Crop yield prediction, Graph Neural Networks, climate similarity, COATI optimization, spatial generalization, SHAP explainability.

    INTRODUCTION
Agriculture remains one of the most critical sectors supporting global food security, economic stability, and human livelihoods. Accurate estimation of crop yield plays a vital role in decision-making processes related to food supply management, trade policy, pricing mechanisms, and climate adaptation strategies. Governments, agricultural planners, and international organizations increasingly rely on predictive models to anticipate production trends, manage supply-chain risks, and respond proactively to climate-induced disruptions. However, reliable crop yield prediction remains a challenging task due to the complex interplay of climatic, environmental, and anthropogenic factors that influence agricultural productivity. Traditionally, crop yield prediction has been approached using statistical regression techniques and process-based crop models. While these methods offer interpretability and domain grounding, they often require extensive domain-specific parameters, high-quality soil and management data, and careful calibration for each geographic region. Such requirements limit scalability and make deployment difficult in data-scarce regions. In recent years, machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) approaches have emerged as powerful alternatives, leveraging historical datasets to model complex non-linear relationships between climate variables and crop yield. Techniques such as Random Forests, Gradient Boosting, and neural networks have demonstrated strong predictive performance on benchmark datasets. Despite these advances, a fundamental limitation persists in most existing crop yield prediction studies: models are typically evaluated using random train-test splits, where data from the same countries appear in both training and testing sets. While this evaluation strategy often produces impressive performance metrics, it fails to reflect real-world deployment scenarios where predictions must be made for regions or countries with limited or no historical yield data. As a result, many models inadvertently learn country-specific or region-specific patterns, leading to severe performance degradation when applied to unseen geographic contexts. This issue highlights the need for prediction frameworks that explicitly address spatial generalization rather than merely optimizing in-distribution accuracy. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) provide a natural and powerful framework for addressing spatial dependencies and relational structures in data. By representing entities as nodes and their relationships as edges, GNNs enable information propagation across interconnected units. In the context of agriculture, countries or regions can be modeled as nodes, with edges encoding similarities or interactions based on geographic proximity, trade relationships, or environmental characteristics. However, naïve graph constructions based solely on geographic adjacency risk reinforcing identity-based memorization rather than facilitating meaningful knowledge transfer. To overcome this limitation, this work proposes a climate-similarity–based graph construction strategy, where countries are connected based on the similarity of their climatic conditions rather than physical proximity or administrative boundaries. Climate variables such as temperature and rainfall directly govern plant physiology, photosynthesis rates, and water availability, making them universal and transferable descriptors of agricultural potential. By constructing graph edges using cosine similarity between climate feature vectors, the proposed approach enables countries with similar agro-climatic profiles to share information, even if they are geographically distant. This design explicitly promotes the transfer of agricultural knowledge across regions with comparable environmental conditions. Another major challenge in crop yield prediction lies in the distributional characteristics of yield data. Crop yields often span several orders of magnitude, with extreme outliers caused by favorable growing conditions, technological interventions, or reporting inconsistencies. Standard regression models trained on raw yield values frequently struggle with unstable gradients and bias toward high-yield regions. In this work, a log normalization strategy is applied to crop yield values prior to model training. This transformation compresses the dynamic range of the target variable, reduces the influence of outliers, and enables more stable and effective learning. Experimental results demonstrate that this preprocessing step is critical, yielding substantial improvements in predictive accuracy across all model variants. In addition to architectural and preprocessing considerations, hyperparameter optimization plays a crucial role in achieving optimal model performance. Manual tuning or grid-based search strategies are often inefficient and computationally expensive, particularly for graph-based models. To address this, the Coati Optimization Algorithm (COATI), a bio-inspired metaheuristic inspired by the hunting and escape behavior of coatis, is employed to optimize key GNN hyperparameters, including learning rate, hidden-layer dimensions, dropout rate, and climate similarity threshold. COATI offers a balance between global exploration and local exploitation, enabling efficient navigation of the hyperparameter search space. However, an important and unexpected finding of this study is that optimization-driven enhancements do not always translate into improved generalization. While COATI-optimized models show marginal gains under random data splits, their performance degrades under strict Leave-Country-Out evaluation. This observation reveals a critical trade-off between semantic enrichment and spatial transferability: optimization mechanisms may inadvertently amplify latent country-specific patterns, undermining robustness in unseen regions. Rather than treating this as a failure, this work frames the finding as a valuable scientific insight into the limitations of optimization-centric model design under domain shift conditions. To further enhance transparency and trustworthiness, SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) are applied to a baseline multilayer perceptron model. SHAP analysis provides feature-level interpretability, revealing how temperature, rainfall, and pesticide usage contribute to yield predictions. The results confirm that the learned relationships align with established agricultural science, demonstrating that the models are not merely memorizing patterns but capturing meaningful climate–yield interactions. The primary contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows: (1) a climate-similarity–driven GNN framework for crop yield prediction that explicitly targets out-of-country generalization, (2) the application of log-normalized yield modeling to stabilize learning and improve predictive performance, (3) a systematic evaluation of COATI-based hyperparameter optimization under strict geographic domain shifts, revealing a fundamental trade-off between optimization and generalization, and (4) an explainability-driven validation using SHAP to ensure scientific plausibility and interpretability. Through these contributions, this work advances the development of robust, transferable, and interpretable crop yield prediction systems suitable for real-world agricultural decision-making.

    LITERATURE SURVEY
A. Anomaly-based Network Intrusion Detection using Denoising Autoencoder and Wasserstein GAN Synthetic Attacks
This work [1] addresses the challenge of detecting zero-day and rare attacks caused by severe class imbalance in intrusion detection datasets. Traditional anomaly-based IDS models struggle to generalize when malicious samples are scarce or unavailable. The authors propose a denoising autoencoder trained on normal traffic patterns, combined with a Wasserstein Generative Adversarial Network (WGAN) to generate synthetic attack samples and balance the dataset. Experimental evaluation on the NSL-KDD and CIC-IDS2017 datasets demonstrates improved detection accuracy and robustness compared to baseline autoencoder-based models. The study highlights the importance of synthetic data generation for improving zero-day detection performance but introduces additional computational complexity due to GAN training.
B. MultiCIDS: Anomaly-based Collective Intrusion Detection by Deep Learning on IoT/CPS Multivariate Time Series
This paper [2] focuses on detecting collective and persistent intrusions in IoT and cyber–physical systems, where isolated anomaly detection often leads to high false-positive rates. The authors propose the MultiCIDS framework, which employs semi-supervised autoencoders trained on normal behavior and integrates sliding-window time-series analysis to capture sustained anomalous patterns. Evaluations conducted on the N-BaIoT, SWaT, and CIC-IDS2017 datasets show high detection accuracy with significantly reduced false alarms. The approach is particularly effective in identifying long-duration zero-day attacks but requires careful selection of window size and system-specific thresholds.
C. HAE-HRL: A Network Intrusion Detection System Utilizing a Novel Autoencoder and Hybrid Enhanced LSTM-CNN Residual Network
This study [3]  addresses feature redundancy and limited representational capacity in deep learning–based intrusion detection systems. The authors introduce a CNN-GRU-based autoencoder for feature selection, followed by a hybrid ResNet–LSTM–CNN architecture enhanced with attention mechanisms. Experimental results on NSL-KDD, UNSW-NB15, and CICIDS-2018 datasets demonstrate superior detection accuracy compared to existing deep IDS models. Although the framework effectively improves feature discrimination and detection accuracy, the hybrid architecture increases computational complexity and may indicate scalability issues in real-time deployments.
D. AI-Driven Exploit Mitigation for Zero-Day Vulnerability Using SVM and Autoencoder
This work [4] investigates the use of supervised and unsupervised learning techniques for detecting zero-day software vulnerabilities. An autoencoder is employed for unsupervised anomaly detection, while Support Vector Machines are used for supervised classification and comparative analysis. Experiments conducted on the Google Zero Days in the Wild dataset show that SVM-based models outperform standalone autoencoders in detection accuracy. The study highlights the strengths of supervised learning for vulnerability classification but also exposes its dependency on labeled data, limiting adaptability to unseen exploits.
E. Deep Autoencoder Model for Intrusion Detection in Financial Cybersecurity Monitoring Systems
This paper [5] targets intrusion detection in financial cybersecurity environments characterized by high-dimensional data and evolving attack patterns. The authors propose a deep autoencoder trained exclusively on normal traffic data, using reconstruction error to identify anomalous behavior. Experimental results demonstrate improved detection accuracy and reduced false-positive rates compared to traditional IDS approaches. While the model proves effective for zero-day detection, its performance depends heavily on the quality of normal training data and appropriate threshold selection.
F. Detecting Zero-Day Attacks using Advanced Anomaly Detection in Network Traffic
This study [6] addresses limitations of autoencoder-only approaches in capturing temporal dependencies within network traffic. The proposed framework integrates autoencoders for feature learning, transformers for temporal modeling, and Isolation Forest for refined anomaly detection. Evaluations on the CSE-CIC-IDS2018 dataset show improved robustness and lower false-positive rates. Despite strong performance, the hybrid architecture introduces higher computational overhead and requires careful parameter tuning.
G. Enhancing Cloud Security with AI-Driven Anomaly Detection for Zero-Day Threats
This paper [7] focuses on intrusion detection in dynamic cloud environments where signature-based methods are ineffective. An unsupervised autoencoder trained on normal traffic is combined with clustering and anomaly detection techniques such as OCSVM, DBSCAN, and Isolation Forest. Results on CIC-IDS2017 and UNSW-NB15 datasets demonstrate high detection accuracy with low false-positive rates. However, the framework is evaluated only on benchmark datasets and requires further validation in real-time cloud deployments.
H. HITL-EdgeSec: A Human-in-the-Loop Autoencoder Framework for Anomaly Detection in 6G Edge Networks
This work [8] addresses the trade-off between detection accuracy and false positives in edge-network intrusion detection. The proposed framework integrates an unsupervised autoencoder with a human-in-the-loop verification mechanism for borderline cases. Evaluation on the CIC-IDS2017 dataset demonstrates improved detection reliability and reduced false alarms. While the approach enhances practicality and trust, it introduces dependency on human analysts, potentially increasing response latency.
I. Unveiling the Unseen: Leveraging Zero-Day Attack Detection Using Unsupervised and Semi-Supervised Learning
This study [9] evaluates unsupervised and semi-supervised techniques for zero-day intrusion detection. A deep autoencoder is used to model normal traffic behavior, and its performance is compared against clustering-based methods such as K-Means and Mean Shift. Results on the NSL-KDD dataset show that autoencoders significantly outperform clustering approaches in detecting unseen attacks. The evaluation, however, is limited to a single dataset, raising concerns regarding generalizability.
J. An Intrusion Detection Method Based on Stacked Sparse Autoencoder and Improved Gaussian Mixture Model
This paper [10] proposes an end-to-end unsupervised intrusion detection framework combining stacked sparse autoencoders with an improved Gaussian Mixture Model. The approach reduces feature redundancy and enhances anomaly discrimination. Experimental results on the UNSW-NB15 dataset show superior performance compared to classical clustering and DAGMM methods. The framework’s multi-stage design increases computational complexity and may require optimization for real-time deployment.
K. A Comprehensive Survey on Intrusion Detection Systems with Advances in Machine Learning and Deep Learning
This paper [11] presents a systematic review of intrusion detection systems, covering signature-based, anomaly-based, and hybrid IDS architectures. The authors analyze machine learning and deep learning techniques, benchmark datasets, and evaluation metrics, highlighting challenges such as scalability, false positives, and adaptability to zero-day attacks. While the survey provides valuable insights and research directions, it does not propose or validate a new IDS model experimentally.
L. A Convolutional Autoencoder Architecture for Robust Network Intrusion Detection in Embedded Systems
This work [12] addresses the deployment of intrusion detection systems on embedded and edge devices with limited resources. A one-dimensional convolutional autoencoder is trained on raw network packets, using reconstruction error for anomaly detection. Experimental results demonstrate high detection accuracy, robustness to poisoning attacks, and low latency. The requirement for raw packet access and careful threshold tuning remain practical challenges.
M. A Framework for Detecting Zero-Day Exploits in Network Flows
This study [13]  proposes a hybrid framework combining supervised classifiers with unsupervised clustering and online learning to detect zero-day exploits. Evaluations on industrial datasets and NSL-KDD show high detection accuracy and reduced false alarms. Although effective, the multi-stage framework introduces higher computational complexity, which may limit scalability in high-speed networks.
N. A New Intrusion Detection Method Using Ensemble Classification and Feature Selection
This paper [14] introduces a hybrid CNN–Random Forest ensemble for intrusion detection, where CNNs perform feature extraction and Random Forest handles classification. Results on KDD99 and UNSW-NB15 datasets demonstrate improved detection accuracy and reduced false positives. The reliance on labeled data limits applicability to zero-day attack scenarios.
O. A Novel Two-Stage Deep Learning Model for Network Intrusion Detection: LSTM-AE
This work [15] proposes a two-stage architecture combining LSTM networks and autoencoders to capture temporal dependencies and structural patterns in network traffic. Experimental evaluation on CICIDS2017 and CSE-CICIDS2018 datasets shows improved detection accuracy compared to traditional models. The model’s high training cost and preprocessing requirements pose scalability challenges.
P. Emerging AI Threats in Cybercrime: A Review of Zero-Day Attacks
This survey [16] examines emerging AI-driven zero-day attacks and defense mechanisms, reviewing machine learning, deep learning, and federated learning approaches. The study highlights privacy, scalability, and adaptability challenges, motivating the development of unsupervised and transferable detection frameworks. The work remains survey-based without experimental validation.
Q. Explainable AI for Zero-Day Attack Detection in IoT Networks Using Attention Fusion Model
This paper [17] addresses interpretability issues in deep IDS models by proposing an attention fusion framework combining Bi-LSTM and self-attention. Explainability is achieved using LIME and SHAP. Results show high detection accuracy and improved transparency. Computational complexity and limited dataset evaluation affect generalizability.
R. Intrusion Detection in IoT Systems Using Denoising Autoencoder
This study [18] proposes a denoising autoencoder trained on normal IoT traffic to detect anomalies via reconstruction error. Evaluations on CICIDS2017 and NSL-KDD datasets demonstrate high robustness and accuracy. The approach depends on threshold selection and struggles with multi-class attack differentiation.
S. Twined Ensemble Framework for Network Security Integrating Multiple Classifiers
This work [19] introduces a twined ensemble framework combining Random Forest, AdaBoost, and Gradient Boosting with soft voting. Results on NSL-KDD demonstrate high accuracy and reliability, particularly for rare attacks. Dependence on labeled data limits adaptability to unseen attack types.
T. Zero-Day Exploits Detection with Adaptive WavePCA-Autoencoder and AHEDNet
This paper [20] proposes a complex hybrid framework integrating adaptive WavePCA-based autoencoders, attention-based transformers, feature optimization, and ensemble learning. Experimental results show high detection accuracy with reduced false positives. However, the framework’s architectural complexity and computational cost pose challenges for real-time deployment.

    METHODOLOGY
The proposed methodology is designed to achieve robust crop yield prediction under strict geographic domain shift, where predictions must be made for countries that are completely unseen during training. Unlike conventional approaches that rely on random data splits and country-specific learning, this framework explicitly targets spatial generalization by combining climate-aware graph modeling with principled preprocessing and optimization strategies. The methodology consists of five major stages: data preprocessing, climate-similarity graph construction, Graph Neural Network (GNN) modeling, COATI-based hyperparameter optimization, and evaluation under Leave-Country-Out validation.
A. Data Collection and Preprocessing
The dataset used in this study consists of country-level agricultural and climatic records collected over multiple years. Each data sample corresponds to a specific country–year pair and includes three primary input features: average temperature, annual rainfall, and pesticide usage. These features were selected due to their global availability and strong biological relevance to crop growth, ensuring applicability in data-scarce regions.
A critical challenge in crop yield prediction is the highly skewed distribution of yield values, which span several orders of magnitude across countries and crops. Directly training regression models on raw yield values leads to unstable gradients and bias toward high-yield regions. To address this issue, a logarithmic normalization strategy is applied to the target variable:
y_"log" =log⁡(y+1)
This transformation compresses the dynamic range of yield values, reduces the influence of extreme outliers, and enables stable gradient-based optimization. Following log transformation, the target values are standardized using z-score normalization. Input features are similarly normalized to ensure consistent scaling across countries and years.
B. Climate-Similarity Graph Construction
To enable structured knowledge transfer across countries, a graph representation is constructed where each country is modeled as a node. Rather than using geographic proximity or administrative boundaries, edges are defined based on climate similarity, reflecting the universal physical principles governing crop growth.
For each country, a climate feature vector is formed using normalized temperature and rainfall values. Pairwise cosine similarity is computed between all country vectors, and edges are established when the similarity exceeds a predefined threshold. This results in an adjacency matrix that connects countries with comparable agro-climatic conditions, even if they are geographically distant.
This climate-similarity graph ensures that message passing in the GNN propagates transferable agricultural knowledge rather than country identity information. As a result, the model can generalize predictions to unseen countries by leveraging patterns learned from climatically similar regions.
C. Graph Neural Network Architecture
The prediction model is based on a two-layer Graph Convolutional Network (GCN). Each node initially encodes the country’s climatic and pesticide features. Through graph convolution operations, each node aggregates information from its climate-similar neighbors, allowing the model to learn shared agricultural patterns. Mathematically, the node embedding at layer l+1is computed as:
H^(lⓜ+1) =σ(D ̃^(-1/2) A ̃D ̃^(-1/2) H^((l) ) W^((l) ))
where A ̃is the climate-similarity adjacency matrix with self-loops, D ̃is the degree matrix, W^((l) )denotes learnable weight matrices, and σis a non-linear activation function. Dropout regularization is applied between layers to mitigate overfitting. The final node embeddings are passed through a regression head to predict log-normalized crop yield.
D. COATI-Based Hyperparameter Optimization
To avoid inefficient manual tuning, the Coati Optimization Algorithm (COATI) is employed to optimize critical GNN hyperparameters, including learning rate, hidden layer dimensions, dropout rate, and climate similarity threshold. COATI is a bio-inspired metaheuristic that alternates between global exploration and local exploitation, mimicking the hunting and escape behavior of coatis.
Each candidate solution represents a set of GNN hyperparameters. The fitness of a solution is evaluated using validation Mean Absolute Error (MAE) under the Leave-Country-Out setting. Through iterative updates, COATI identifies hyperparameter configurations that minimize validation error. While COATI improves in-distribution performance, its effect on out-of-country generalization is carefully analyzed to avoid overfitting.
E. Leave-Country-Out Validation Protocol
To rigorously evaluate spatial generalization, a Leave-Country-Out (LCO) validation strategy is adopted. In this setting, entire countries are excluded from training and used only for testing. This protocol prevents information leakage and ensures that the model’s performance reflects its ability to generalize to unseen regions.
Model performance is assessed using Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and coefficient of determination (R²). This evaluation strategy provides a realistic measure of deployment readiness, particularly for regions with limited historical data.
